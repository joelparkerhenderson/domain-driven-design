# Agile - Ubiquitous Language

Sprint: A time-boxed iteration (typically 1-4 weeks) during which a team delivers a potentially shippable product increment.

Product Backlog: An ordered list of everything known to be needed in a product, owned and prioritized by the Product Owner.

Sprint Backlog: The subset of product backlog items selected for a sprint, plus the team's plan for delivering them.

User Story: A short, informal description of a feature from the perspective of an end user, typically following "As a [role], I want [goal], so that [benefit]."

Epic: A large body of work that can be broken down into multiple user stories spanning one or more sprints.

Task: A specific unit of work required to complete a user story, typically estimated in hours.

Story Point: A relative unit of measure for estimating the effort, complexity, and uncertainty of a user story.

Velocity: The average number of story points a team completes per sprint, used for forecasting.

Burndown Chart: A visual representation of remaining work versus time in a sprint or release.

Burnup Chart: A visual representation of completed work versus total scope over time.

Product Owner: The person responsible for maximizing product value by managing and prioritizing the product backlog.

Scrum Master: A servant-leader who facilitates Scrum events, removes impediments, and coaches the team on agile practices.

Development Team: A cross-functional, self-organizing group of professionals who deliver the product increment.

Sprint Planning: A ceremony where the team selects backlog items and creates a plan for the upcoming sprint.

Daily Standup: A brief daily meeting (typically 15 minutes) where team members share progress, plans, and blockers.

Sprint Review: A ceremony at sprint end where the team demonstrates completed work to stakeholders for feedback.

Sprint Retrospective: A ceremony where the team reflects on the sprint to identify improvements for the next iteration.

Backlog Refinement: An ongoing activity of reviewing, estimating, and clarifying product backlog items before sprint planning.

Definition of Done: A shared checklist of criteria that a user story or increment must meet to be considered complete.

Definition of Ready: A shared checklist of criteria that a backlog item must meet before it can be selected for a sprint.

Acceptance Criteria: Specific conditions that a user story must satisfy to be accepted by the Product Owner.

Impediment: An obstacle that prevents the team from making progress, requiring Scrum Master intervention.

Increment: The sum of all product backlog items completed during a sprint and all previous sprints.

Release: A deployment of one or more increments to users or production, potentially spanning multiple sprints.

Kanban Board: A visual workflow management tool showing work items moving through defined stages (To Do, In Progress, Done).

WIP Limit: Work In Progress limit — the maximum number of items allowed in a workflow stage to prevent overload.

Cycle Time: The elapsed time from when work begins on an item to when it is completed.

Lead Time: The elapsed time from when an item is requested to when it is delivered.

Throughput: The number of work items completed in a given time period.

Cumulative Flow Diagram: A chart showing the quantity of items in each workflow state over time, revealing bottlenecks.

Continuous Integration: The practice of frequently merging code changes into a shared repository with automated builds and tests.

Continuous Delivery: The practice of keeping software in a deployable state at all times through automation.

Feature Flag: A configuration mechanism to enable or disable features without deploying new code.

Technical Debt: The accumulated cost of shortcuts or suboptimal solutions that must eventually be addressed.

Spike: A time-boxed research task to investigate a question or reduce uncertainty before committing to implementation.

Planning Poker: A consensus-based estimation technique using cards to vote on story point values.

MoSCoW: A prioritization technique categorizing items as Must have, Should have, Could have, Won't have.

SAFe: Scaled Agile Framework — a framework for scaling agile practices across large enterprises.

Scrum: An agile framework for managing work through sprints with defined roles, events, and artifacts.

Kanban: An agile method focused on visualizing workflow, limiting work in progress, and optimizing flow.

XP: Extreme Programming — an agile methodology emphasizing engineering practices like pair programming and TDD.

Action Item: A specific improvement task identified during a retrospective, assigned to a team member for follow-through.

Capacity: The amount of available work effort a team has for a given sprint, accounting for holidays and absences.
