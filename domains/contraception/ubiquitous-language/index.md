# Contraception - Ubiquitous Language

- **Barrier Method**: A contraceptive method that physically prevents sperm from reaching the ovum, including male and female condoms, diaphragms, and cervical caps.

- **Combined Hormonal Contraceptive (CHC)**: A contraceptive method containing both estrogen and progestogen, available as oral pills, transdermal patches, or vaginal rings, that prevents ovulation and alters cervical mucus.

- **Continuation Rate**: The proportion of patients who continue using a contraceptive method over a defined period, used as a measure of method acceptability and satisfaction.

- **Contraceptive Counseling**: A structured patient-centered conversation in which a clinician discusses available contraceptive options, effectiveness, side effects, and lifestyle considerations to support informed method selection.

- **Contraceptive Episode**: A continuous period during which a patient uses a specific contraceptive method, beginning at initiation and ending at discontinuation, removal, or switching to a different method.

- **Copper Intrauterine Device (Cu-IUD)**: A non-hormonal long-acting reversible contraceptive device inserted into the uterus that releases copper ions to create a spermicidal environment and prevent fertilization.

- **Drug Interaction Screening**: The clinical process of evaluating whether a patient's concurrent medications may reduce contraceptive efficacy or be affected by hormonal contraceptives.

- **Effectiveness Rate**: The measured success of a contraceptive method at preventing pregnancy, expressed as perfect-use and typical-use failure rates per 100 woman-years.

- **Emergency Contraception (EC)**: A method used after unprotected intercourse or contraceptive failure to prevent pregnancy, including oral levonorgestrel, ulipristal acetate, or copper IUD insertion.

- **Hormonal Intrauterine System (IUS)**: A long-acting reversible contraceptive device inserted into the uterus that releases a progestogen (levonorgestrel) locally to thin the endometrium and thicken cervical mucus.

- **Implant**: A small flexible rod containing a progestogen (etonogestrel) inserted subdermally in the upper arm that provides long-acting reversible contraception for up to three years.

- **Long-Acting Reversible Contraception (LARC)**: Contraceptive methods that provide effective pregnancy prevention for an extended period without requiring daily user action, including intrauterine devices, intrauterine systems, and subdermal implants.

- **MEC Category**: One of four WHO Medical Eligibility Criteria categories (1 through 4) classifying the safety of a contraceptive method for a patient with a specific medical condition, from no restriction to unacceptable health risk.

- **Method Switching**: The clinical process of transitioning a patient from one contraceptive method to another, including overlap or bridging strategies to maintain continuous pregnancy prevention.

- **Pearl Index**: A statistical measure of contraceptive failure expressed as the number of unintended pregnancies per 100 woman-years of contraceptive use.

- **Progestogen-Only Pill (POP)**: An oral contraceptive containing only a progestogen that primarily acts by thickening cervical mucus and, in some formulations, inhibiting ovulation.

- **Quick Start**: A prescribing approach in which a patient begins a new contraceptive method on the day of the consultation rather than waiting for the next menstrual period, after excluding current pregnancy.

- **Removal**: The clinical procedure of extracting an intrauterine device or subdermal implant, performed either at the scheduled end of the device's licensed duration or at the patient's request.

- **Side Effect**: An unintended physiological response to a contraceptive method, such as changes in bleeding pattern, headache, mood changes, or weight change, that may affect continuation.

- **Typical-Use Failure Rate**: The probability of unintended pregnancy during the first year of contraceptive use under real-world conditions, accounting for inconsistent or incorrect use.
