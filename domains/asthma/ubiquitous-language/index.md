# Asthma Domain - Ubiquitous Language

This glossary defines the shared vocabulary used across all bounded contexts in the asthma management domain. These terms ensure consistent communication between clinicians, respiratory therapists, patients, developers, and stakeholders.

- **Spirometry**: A pulmonary function test that measures the volume and speed of air a patient can inhale and exhale, used as the primary diagnostic and monitoring tool for asthma.
- **Peak Expiratory Flow (PEF)**: The maximum speed of expiration measured by a peak flow meter, used for daily self-monitoring and action plan zone determination.
- **Fractional Exhaled Nitric Oxide (FeNO)**: A biomarker measurement of airway inflammation levels, indicating eosinophilic inflammation and guiding treatment decisions.
- **Forced Expiratory Volume in One Second (FEV1)**: The volume of air forcibly exhaled in the first second of a spirometry test, serving as a key indicator of airway obstruction severity.
- **FEV1/FVC Ratio**: The ratio of forced expiratory volume in one second to forced vital capacity, used to confirm obstructive airway disease when below the lower limit of normal.
- **GINA Classification**: The Global Initiative for Asthma severity and control classification system that categorizes asthma as intermittent, mild persistent, moderate persistent, or severe persistent.
- **Asthma Control Test (ACT)**: A validated five-question patient questionnaire that produces a score from 5 to 25, where scores below 20 indicate inadequately controlled asthma.
- **Stepwise Therapy**: The GINA-recommended approach to asthma treatment that escalates or de-escalates medication intensity based on symptom control and risk assessment.
- **Controller Medication**: A daily maintenance medication such as an inhaled corticosteroid taken regularly to reduce airway inflammation and prevent asthma symptoms.
- **Rescue Inhaler**: A short-acting beta-agonist (SABA) bronchodilator used for rapid relief of acute asthma symptoms and bronchospasm.
- **Inhaled Corticosteroid (ICS)**: The cornerstone anti-inflammatory controller medication delivered directly to the airways via inhalation device.
- **Long-Acting Beta-Agonist (LABA)**: A bronchodilator medication that provides sustained airway relaxation for 12 or more hours, always used in combination with an ICS.
- **Biologic Agent**: A targeted monoclonal antibody therapy (such as omalizumab, mepolizumab, or dupilumab) used for severe asthma that remains uncontrolled on standard stepwise therapy.
- **Allergen Immunotherapy**: A treatment that gradually desensitizes the immune system to specific allergens through subcutaneous injections or sublingual tablets.
- **Asthma Action Plan**: A personalized written document developed with a clinician that instructs the patient on daily management, symptom recognition, and emergency response.
- **Zone System**: The traffic-light classification within an asthma action plan where green indicates well-controlled asthma, yellow indicates caution with worsening symptoms, and red indicates a medical emergency.
- **Exacerbation**: A clinically significant worsening of asthma symptoms and lung function that requires a change in treatment, such as systemic corticosteroids or emergency care.
- **Trigger**: An environmental, occupational, or physiological factor that provokes airway inflammation or bronchospasm, including allergens, irritants, exercise, and infections.
- **Air Quality Index (AQI)**: A standardized numerical scale that communicates outdoor air pollution levels and their potential impact on respiratory health.
- **Occupational Asthma**: Asthma caused or worsened by workplace exposures to sensitizers or irritants, requiring specialized monitoring and environmental controls.
- **Medication Adherence**: The degree to which a patient follows the prescribed medication regimen, measured through pharmacy refill data, inhaler counters, or electronic monitoring devices.
- **Inhaler Technique**: The specific sequence of steps required to correctly use an inhalation device, where poor technique significantly reduces medication delivery to the airways.
- **Bronchial Hyperresponsiveness**: An exaggerated bronchoconstrictor response to stimuli such as methacholine or exercise, characteristic of asthma pathophysiology.
- **Eosinophilic Inflammation**: A type of airway inflammation characterized by elevated eosinophil counts, often responsive to corticosteroids and biologic agents targeting IL-5 or IL-4/IL-13.
- **Phenotype**: A clinically observable characteristic pattern of asthma presentation (such as allergic, exercise-induced, or late-onset) that guides treatment selection.
- **Endotype**: The underlying biological mechanism driving a specific asthma phenotype, such as Type 2 (T2-high) or non-Type 2 (T2-low) inflammation.
- **Lung Function Trend**: The longitudinal trajectory of pulmonary function measurements over time, used to detect progressive decline or improvement in airway function.
- **Quality of Life Score**: A standardized patient-reported outcome measure (such as AQLQ) that quantifies the impact of asthma on daily activities, emotional well-being, and symptom burden.
- **Emergency Protocol**: A predefined clinical response pathway activated when a patient enters the red zone, specifying rescue medication dosing, emergency contacts, and criteria for seeking immediate medical care.
- **Shared Decision-Making**: A collaborative process between clinician and patient for determining treatment goals, action plan parameters, and therapy adjustments based on individual preferences and clinical evidence.
