# Energy - Ubiquitous Language

Generating Unit: A single power-producing facility or machine, such as a gas turbine, steam turbine, or solar array, identified by a unique unit ID.

Capacity: The maximum power output a generating unit or plant can produce, measured in megawatts (MW).

Dispatch: The process of directing a generating unit to produce a specific amount of power at a given time to meet grid demand.

Heat Rate: The efficiency of a thermal generating unit, expressed as the amount of fuel energy (BTU) required to produce one kilowatt-hour of electricity.

Load: The total amount of electrical power demanded by consumers on the grid at a given point in time.

Load Profile: A time-series representation of electricity consumption or demand over a specific period, typically in 15-minute or hourly intervals.

Base Load: The minimum level of demand on the grid over a 24-hour period, typically served by low-cost, continuously running generating units.

Peak Load: The maximum level of demand on the grid during a specific period, requiring additional generating units to be dispatched.

Megawatt-Hour (MWh): A unit of energy equal to one megawatt of power sustained for one hour, used as the standard unit for energy trading and billing.

Locational Marginal Price (LMP): The price of electricity at a specific node on the transmission grid, reflecting generation cost, congestion, and losses.

Day-Ahead Market: A wholesale energy market where electricity is traded for delivery on the following day, with prices set by supply and demand bids.

Real-Time Market: A wholesale energy market that balances supply and demand in near real-time, typically in 5-minute intervals.

Energy Contract: A financial or physical agreement to buy or sell a specified quantity of energy at a defined price over a defined period.

Power Purchase Agreement (PPA): A long-term contract between an electricity generator and a buyer specifying price, quantity, and delivery terms.

Settlement: The financial reconciliation process that calculates amounts owed between market participants based on actual generation, consumption, and market prices.

Transmission Line: A high-voltage conductor that carries electricity from generating stations to substations and load centers across the grid.

Substation: A facility that transforms voltage levels, switches circuits, and routes power flow within the transmission and distribution network.

Distribution Feeder: A medium-voltage circuit that delivers electricity from a distribution substation to end-use customers.

Outage: An interruption in the supply of electricity, either planned (for maintenance) or unplanned (due to equipment failure or weather events).

Fault: An abnormal electrical condition on the grid, such as a short circuit, that can cause protective equipment to isolate the affected section.

SCADA: Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition -- a system for monitoring and controlling grid equipment in real time.

Meter: A device that measures the quantity of electricity consumed or generated at a specific point, recording data for billing and analytics.

Smart Meter: An advanced meter capable of two-way communication, recording consumption at granular intervals and transmitting data remotely.

Meter Reading: A recorded measurement of electricity consumption or generation at a specific meter during a specific time interval.

Tariff: A published schedule of rates, charges, and terms under which a utility provides electricity service to its customers.

Billing Cycle: The recurring period (typically monthly) over which electricity consumption is measured and billed to a customer.

Demand Response: A program that incentivizes or requires electricity consumers to reduce or shift their consumption during peak demand periods.

Ancillary Services: Support services required to maintain grid reliability, including frequency regulation, spinning reserves, and voltage support.

Frequency Regulation: The continuous adjustment of generation output to maintain grid frequency at 60 Hz (North America) or 50 Hz (Europe/Asia).

Spinning Reserve: Generation capacity that is online, synchronized to the grid, and available to respond to sudden demand increases or generation losses within seconds.

Curtailment: The intentional reduction of electricity output from a generating unit, typically applied to renewable sources when supply exceeds demand or grid capacity.

Renewable Energy Certificate (REC): A tradeable certificate representing proof that one megawatt-hour of electricity was generated from a renewable energy source.

Distributed Energy Resource (DER): A small-scale energy generation or storage device located at or near the point of consumption, such as rooftop solar or a battery system.

Interconnection: The process and physical infrastructure by which a generating unit or DER connects to the electric grid.

Net Metering: A billing mechanism that credits DER owners for electricity they export to the grid, offsetting their consumption charges.

Battery Energy Storage System (BESS): A system that stores electrical energy in batteries for later dispatch, used for grid balancing, peak shaving, and renewable integration.

State of Charge (SOC): The current energy level of a battery storage system expressed as a percentage of its total capacity.

Capacity Factor: The ratio of actual energy produced by a generating unit over a period to the maximum possible energy it could have produced if operating at full capacity continuously.

Grid Congestion: A condition where transmission capacity is insufficient to deliver all requested power flows, resulting in price differences across the grid.

Balancing Authority: An entity responsible for maintaining the balance between electricity supply and demand within a defined geographic area.

Independent System Operator (ISO): A regional organization that coordinates, controls, and monitors the electric grid and administers wholesale electricity markets.

Regional Transmission Organization (RTO): A type of ISO that manages the transmission grid and wholesale markets across a multi-state region.

Node: A specific point on the transmission grid where electricity is injected (generation) or withdrawn (load), used to calculate locational prices.

Power Factor: The ratio of real power (watts) to apparent power (volt-amperes) in an AC circuit, indicating how efficiently electrical power is being used.

Voltage: The electrical potential difference that drives current through a conductor, measured in volts or kilovolts on the grid.
