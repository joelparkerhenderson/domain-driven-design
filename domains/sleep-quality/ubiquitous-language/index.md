# Sleep Quality Domain - Ubiquitous Language

Sleep Efficiency: The percentage of time spent asleep relative to the total time spent in bed, serving as a primary metric for sleep quality.

Sleep Onset Latency: The duration of time it takes an individual to transition from full wakefulness to sleep after lights out.

Wake After Sleep Onset (WASO): The total amount of time a person spends awake after initially falling asleep but before final awakening.

Total Sleep Time: The cumulative duration of actual sleep obtained during a sleep period, excluding periods of wakefulness.

Sleep Architecture: The structural organization of sleep into distinct stages (N1, N2, N3, REM) and their cyclical patterns throughout the night.

Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index (PSQI): A validated self-report questionnaire that measures sleep quality and disturbances over a one-month period, yielding a global score from 0 to 21.

Epworth Sleepiness Scale (ESS): A validated questionnaire that measures an individual's general level of daytime sleepiness by rating the likelihood of dozing in eight common situations.

Polysomnography (PSG): A comprehensive multi-parametric sleep study that records brain waves, blood oxygen, heart rate, breathing, and eye and leg movements during sleep.

Actigraphy: A non-invasive method of monitoring rest and activity cycles using a wrist-worn device that estimates sleep-wake patterns over extended periods.

Sleep Diary: A daily self-report log in which individuals record bedtime, wake time, sleep onset latency, nighttime awakenings, and subjective sleep quality.

Insomnia: A sleep disorder characterized by persistent difficulty initiating sleep, maintaining sleep, or experiencing non-restorative sleep despite adequate opportunity.

Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA): A disorder in which the upper airway repeatedly collapses during sleep, causing breathing interruptions and oxygen desaturation.

Apnea-Hypopnea Index (AHI): The number of apnea and hypopnea events per hour of sleep, used to classify the severity of sleep apnea.

Restless Legs Syndrome (RLS): A neurological sensorimotor disorder characterized by an irresistible urge to move the legs, typically accompanied by uncomfortable sensations.

Narcolepsy: A chronic neurological disorder affecting the brain's ability to regulate sleep-wake cycles, often causing excessive daytime sleepiness and sudden sleep attacks.

Parasomnia: A category of sleep disorders involving abnormal movements, behaviors, emotions, perceptions, or dreams that occur during sleep transitions or sleep stages.

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia (CBT-I): An evidence-based structured program that addresses the thoughts and behaviors contributing to insomnia through multiple therapeutic components.

Sleep Restriction Therapy: A behavioral intervention that limits time in bed to match actual sleep time, creating mild sleep deprivation to consolidate sleep and increase sleep efficiency.

Stimulus Control: A behavioral technique that strengthens the association between the bed and sleep by establishing rules about bed use and bedroom behavior.

Sleep Hygiene: A set of behavioral and environmental recommendations designed to promote healthy sleep habits and optimal sleep quality.

Circadian Rhythm: The approximately 24-hour internal biological clock that regulates the sleep-wake cycle and other physiological processes.

Melatonin: A hormone produced by the pineal gland that signals darkness to the body and plays a key role in regulating the timing of sleep onset.

CPAP (Continuous Positive Airway Pressure): A therapeutic device that delivers pressurized air through a mask to keep the upper airway open during sleep in patients with obstructive sleep apnea.

Sleep Stage: One of the distinct phases of sleep (N1, N2, N3 non-REM, or REM) characterized by specific patterns of brain activity, eye movement, and muscle tone.

Sleep Fragmentation: The disruption of sleep continuity by brief arousals or awakenings, which degrades sleep quality even when total sleep time appears adequate.

Daytime Functioning: The impact of sleep quality on an individual's cognitive performance, mood, energy levels, and ability to carry out daily activities.

Sleep Environment: The physical conditions of the sleeping space including light levels, ambient noise, temperature, air quality, and bedding characteristics.

Sleep Quality Score: A composite numerical value derived from multiple sleep parameters that provides an overall assessment of sleep quality for a given period.

Intervention Protocol: A structured treatment plan specifying the type, dosage, schedule, and duration of a behavioral or clinical intervention for a sleep disorder.

Outcomes Trend: A longitudinal view of sleep quality metrics over time that reveals patterns of improvement, deterioration, or stability in response to interventions.
