# Attention-Deficit Domain: Ubiquitous Language

Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD): A neurodevelopmental disorder characterized by persistent patterns of inattention, hyperactivity, and impulsivity that interfere with functioning or development.

Inattention: A core symptom dimension involving difficulty sustaining focus, following through on tasks, and organizing activities.

Hyperactivity: A core symptom dimension involving excessive motor activity, fidgeting, and difficulty remaining still in structured settings.

Impulsivity: A core symptom dimension involving hasty actions without forethought, difficulty waiting, and interrupting others.

Executive Function: A set of cognitive processes including working memory, cognitive flexibility, and inhibitory control that are commonly impaired in ADHD.

DSM-5 Diagnostic Criteria: The standardized diagnostic framework from the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, Fifth Edition, used to formally diagnose ADHD.

ADHD Presentation Type: The classification of ADHD into predominantly inattentive, predominantly hyperactive-impulsive, or combined presentation based on symptom patterns.

Rating Scale: A standardized questionnaire such as the Conners or SNAP-IV used to quantify ADHD symptom severity across settings.

Conners Rating Scale: A widely used multi-informant assessment tool that measures ADHD symptoms and related behavioral problems in children and adolescents.

SNAP-IV: The Swanson, Nolan, and Pelham rating scale that directly maps to DSM criteria for ADHD symptom assessment.

Continuous Performance Test (CPT): A neuropsychological test measuring sustained attention, response inhibition, and vigilance over time.

Neuropsychological Assessment: A comprehensive evaluation of cognitive functions including attention, memory, processing speed, and executive function.

Multimodal Treatment: An integrated treatment approach combining medication, behavioral therapy, psychoeducation, and environmental modifications.

Behavioral Therapy: A structured therapeutic intervention that uses reinforcement strategies to modify maladaptive behaviors and build adaptive skills.

Parent Training: A structured program teaching parents behavior management techniques, positive reinforcement strategies, and consistent discipline approaches.

Coaching Program: A structured support relationship focused on developing organizational skills, time management, and goal-directed behavior in individuals with ADHD.

Stimulant Medication: First-line pharmacological treatment for ADHD, including methylphenidate and amphetamine-based medications that increase dopamine and norepinephrine activity.

Non-Stimulant Medication: Alternative pharmacological treatments for ADHD, including atomoxetine, guanfacine, and clonidine, used when stimulants are ineffective or contraindicated.

Titration: The systematic process of adjusting medication dosage to achieve optimal symptom control with minimal side effects.

Side Effect Profile: The documented range of adverse effects associated with a specific medication, monitored throughout the treatment episode.

Individualized Education Program (IEP): A legally binding educational plan developed under IDEA for students with disabilities, including ADHD, that specifies accommodations and special education services.

504 Plan: An educational accommodation plan developed under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act that provides modifications and supports for students with ADHD in general education settings.

Classroom Accommodation: A modification to the learning environment, instruction, or assessment designed to support a student with ADHD without altering curriculum standards.

Behavior Intervention Plan (BIP): A proactive plan that identifies target behaviors, antecedent strategies, and consequence-based interventions for managing ADHD-related behavioral challenges.

Symptom Tracking: The systematic recording of ADHD symptom frequency, duration, and severity across settings and time periods.

Functional Impairment: The degree to which ADHD symptoms interfere with an individual's ability to perform daily activities across academic, social, and occupational domains.

Treatment Response: The measurable change in symptom severity and functional impairment following the initiation or modification of a treatment intervention.

Quality of Life Measure: A standardized assessment of an individual's overall well-being, satisfaction, and functioning across physical, emotional, and social domains in the context of ADHD.

Comorbidity: A co-occurring condition alongside ADHD, such as anxiety, depression, oppositional defiant disorder, or learning disabilities, that influences assessment and treatment planning.

Clinical Practice Guideline: An evidence-based recommendation from professional organizations such as the AAP or NICE that informs standardized approaches to ADHD diagnosis and treatment.
