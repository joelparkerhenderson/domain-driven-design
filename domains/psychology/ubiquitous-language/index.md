# Psychology Domain - Ubiquitous Language

Psychometric Test: A standardized instrument designed to measure psychological constructs such as intelligence, personality, or symptom severity with established reliability and validity.

Test Battery: A coordinated set of psychological tests administered together to provide a comprehensive evaluation of an individual's cognitive, emotional, or behavioral functioning.

Normative Data: Statistical reference data derived from a representative population sample against which individual test scores are compared and interpreted.

Clinical Interview: A structured or semi-structured conversation conducted by a clinician to gather diagnostic information, history, and presenting concerns from a client.

Treatment Protocol: A standardized, evidence-based sequence of therapeutic interventions designed to address a specific psychological condition or set of symptoms.

Therapeutic Alliance: The collaborative, trusting relationship between a therapist and client that research consistently identifies as a key predictor of treatment outcomes.

Session Note: A clinical documentation record capturing the content, interventions used, client responses, and progress observed during a therapy session.

Informed Consent: The ethical and legal process of ensuring a client understands and voluntarily agrees to participate in assessment, treatment, or research.

Functional Behavior Assessment: A systematic process of identifying the environmental antecedents and consequences that maintain a target behavior to inform intervention design.

Behavior Intervention Plan: A structured document specifying target behaviors, replacement behaviors, reinforcement strategies, and data collection procedures derived from a functional assessment.

Applied Behavior Analysis: A scientific discipline that applies principles of learning and behavior to produce socially significant improvements in behavior through systematic intervention.

Reinforcement Schedule: A rule specifying the conditions under which a response will be followed by a reinforcer, governing the rate and pattern of behavior.

Cognitive Screening: A brief standardized assessment designed to detect possible cognitive impairment and determine whether comprehensive neuropsychological evaluation is warranted.

Executive Function: A set of higher-order cognitive processes including planning, working memory, inhibition, and cognitive flexibility that regulate goal-directed behavior.

Neuropsychological Assessment: A comprehensive evaluation of cognitive functioning using standardized tests to characterize brain-behavior relationships and guide treatment planning.

Cognitive Profile: A summary representation of an individual's strengths and weaknesses across multiple cognitive domains derived from standardized assessment.

Evidence-Based Practice: The integration of the best available research evidence with clinical expertise and client values and preferences in making treatment decisions.

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy: A structured, time-limited psychotherapy that addresses maladaptive thought patterns and behaviors through cognitive restructuring and behavioral activation.

Acceptance and Commitment Therapy: A third-wave behavioral therapy that uses acceptance, mindfulness, and values-based action to increase psychological flexibility.

EMDR: Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing, a structured psychotherapy that uses bilateral stimulation to process traumatic memories and reduce associated distress.

Diagnostic Formulation: A clinician's integrated understanding of a client's presenting problems, incorporating diagnostic impressions, contributing factors, and treatment implications.

Patient-Reported Outcome: A standardized measure completed directly by the client to capture their subjective experience of symptoms, functioning, and quality of life.

Treatment Effectiveness: The degree to which a therapeutic intervention produces the intended clinical outcomes when delivered in real-world practice settings.

Benchmarking: The process of comparing individual or program-level treatment outcomes against established population norms or best-practice standards.

IRB Protocol: An Institutional Review Board-approved document specifying the design, procedures, risks, and ethical safeguards of a research study involving human participants.

Statistical Significance: A determination that observed research results are unlikely to have occurred by chance alone, typically assessed against an alpha threshold of 0.05.

Effect Size: A quantitative measure of the magnitude of a treatment effect or research finding, independent of sample size, enabling comparison across studies.

Reliability: The degree to which a psychological measure produces consistent, reproducible results across repeated administrations, raters, or items.

Validity: The degree to which a psychological measure accurately assesses the construct it purports to measure, supported by multiple forms of evidence.

Outcome Measure: A standardized instrument used to quantify change in a client's symptoms, functioning, or well-being over the course of treatment.
