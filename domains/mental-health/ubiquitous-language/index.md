# Mental Health Domain - Ubiquitous Language

Assessment: A structured clinical evaluation that gathers information about a patient's mental health symptoms, history, and functioning to inform diagnosis and treatment.

Biopsychosocial Model: An integrated framework that considers biological, psychological, and social factors when understanding and treating mental health conditions.

Care Level: The intensity of mental health services a patient receives, ranging from outpatient therapy to partial hospitalization to inpatient psychiatric care.

Clinical Formulation: A clinician's explanatory narrative that integrates assessment findings into a coherent understanding of the patient's presenting problems and contributing factors.

Comorbidity: The simultaneous presence of two or more mental health diagnoses or the co-occurrence of a mental health condition with a medical condition in the same patient.

Crisis Intervention: An immediate, short-term response to a patient experiencing an acute psychiatric emergency such as suicidal ideation, psychosis, or severe self-harm.

Diagnostic Criteria: The specific set of symptoms, duration requirements, and functional impairment thresholds defined by classification systems like the DSM-5 that must be met for a formal diagnosis.

Domain Event: A significant occurrence within the mental health system that other bounded contexts may need to react to, such as a completed assessment triggering treatment plan creation.

Drug Interaction: A clinically significant effect that occurs when a psychotropic medication alters the efficacy or toxicity of another medication the patient is taking.

DSM-5: The Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, Fifth Edition, published by the American Psychiatric Association, serving as the primary classification system for mental health diagnoses.

Evidence-Based Practice: Clinical decision-making that integrates the best available research evidence with clinical expertise and patient values and preferences.

Functional Assessment: An evaluation of a patient's ability to perform daily activities, maintain relationships, and fulfill role obligations, used to gauge the real-world impact of mental health symptoms.

GAD-7: The Generalized Anxiety Disorder 7-item scale, a validated self-report questionnaire used to screen for and measure the severity of generalized anxiety disorder.

Informed Consent: The process by which a patient voluntarily agrees to treatment after receiving adequate information about the nature, risks, benefits, and alternatives of the proposed intervention.

Intake: The initial administrative and clinical process when a patient first enters the mental health system, including registration, insurance verification, and preliminary screening.

Modality: A specific type or method of therapeutic intervention, such as Cognitive Behavioral Therapy, Dialectical Behavior Therapy, or Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing.

Outcome Measure: A standardized instrument or metric used to evaluate the effectiveness of treatment by quantifying changes in symptoms, functioning, or quality of life over time.

Patient-Reported Outcome: Health status information provided directly by the patient without interpretation by a clinician, typically captured through validated questionnaires or structured interviews.

PHQ-9: The Patient Health Questionnaire 9-item scale, a validated self-report instrument used to screen for, diagnose, and monitor the severity of major depressive disorder.

Progress Note: A clinical document written after each therapy session that records the patient's presentation, interventions used, patient response, and plan for subsequent sessions.

Psychotropic Medication: A pharmaceutical agent that acts on the central nervous system to alter mood, cognition, perception, or behavior, including antidepressants, anxiolytics, antipsychotics, and mood stabilizers.

Risk Assessment: A systematic evaluation of a patient's potential for harm to self or others, considering risk factors, protective factors, and warning signs to determine the appropriate level of intervention.

Safety Plan: A written, prioritized list of coping strategies and resources that a patient can use during a crisis to reduce the risk of self-harm or suicide.

Screening: A brief, standardized process used to identify individuals who may be at risk for a mental health condition and who warrant further comprehensive assessment.

Session: A scheduled, time-bounded encounter between a patient and a mental health provider during which therapeutic work occurs.

Symptom Tracking: The systematic, repeated measurement of specific mental health symptoms over time to monitor treatment response, detect relapse, and inform clinical decisions.

Therapeutic Alliance: The collaborative, trusting relationship between patient and therapist that research consistently identifies as one of the strongest predictors of positive treatment outcomes.

Titration: The gradual, systematic adjustment of psychotropic medication dosage to find the minimum effective dose that achieves therapeutic benefit while minimizing side effects.

Treatment Goal: A specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound objective collaboratively set by the patient and clinician that defines what successful treatment will accomplish.

Treatment Plan: A comprehensive, individualized document that specifies the patient's diagnoses, treatment goals, selected interventions, responsible providers, and expected timelines for review.
