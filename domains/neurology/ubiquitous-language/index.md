# Neurology Domain - Ubiquitous Language

Neurological Examination: A systematic clinical assessment of the nervous system evaluating mental status, cranial nerves, motor function, sensory function, reflexes, coordination, and gait.

Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS): A standardized scoring system from 3 to 15 that quantifies a patient's level of consciousness based on eye opening, verbal response, and motor response.

Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE): A 30-point cognitive screening tool that assesses orientation, registration, attention, recall, and language to detect cognitive impairment.

Montreal Cognitive Assessment (MoCA): A validated cognitive screening instrument designed to detect mild cognitive impairment across multiple domains including visuospatial, executive function, naming, memory, attention, language, abstraction, and orientation.

Cranial Nerve Assessment: The systematic evaluation of all twelve cranial nerves to identify focal neurological deficits that localize lesions within the brainstem or peripheral nerve pathways.

Reflex Grade: A standardized scale from 0 (absent) to 4+ (clonus) used to document the briskness of deep tendon reflexes during neurological examination.

Electroencephalogram (EEG): A non-invasive neurophysiological recording of electrical activity in the brain used to diagnose epilepsy, encephalopathy, and other cerebral disorders.

Electromyography (EMG): A diagnostic technique that records the electrical activity of skeletal muscles to evaluate neuromuscular function and differentiate between myopathic and neuropathic conditions.

Nerve Conduction Study (NCS): A diagnostic test that measures the speed and amplitude of electrical signal propagation along peripheral nerves to assess nerve integrity and function.

Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI): An advanced neuroimaging modality that uses magnetic fields and radio waves to produce detailed images of brain and spinal cord structures without ionizing radiation.

Computed Tomography (CT): A rapid neuroimaging technique using X-ray cross-sections to detect acute hemorrhage, fractures, and mass lesions in emergency neurological presentations.

Positron Emission Tomography (PET): A functional neuroimaging technique that uses radioactive tracers to measure metabolic activity, amyloid burden, or dopaminergic function in the brain.

Seizure Classification: The ILAE system that categorizes seizures by onset type (focal, generalized, unknown), awareness level, and motor versus non-motor manifestations.

Antiepileptic Drug (AED): A medication used to prevent or reduce the frequency and severity of epileptic seizures, selected based on seizure type, patient profile, and side effect considerations.

Vagus Nerve Stimulator (VNS): An implantable neuromodulation device that delivers electrical impulses to the vagus nerve to reduce seizure frequency in drug-resistant epilepsy.

Epilepsy Monitoring Unit (EMU): A specialized inpatient facility where continuous video-EEG monitoring is performed to capture and classify seizures for surgical candidacy evaluation.

Neurodegenerative Disease: A category of disorders characterized by progressive loss of structure or function of neurons, including Alzheimer's disease, Parkinson's disease, ALS, and multiple sclerosis.

Unified Parkinson's Disease Rating Scale (UPDRS): A comprehensive clinical rating scale used to assess the severity and progression of Parkinson's disease across motor and non-motor domains.

Amyloid Cascade: The pathological process in Alzheimer's disease involving accumulation of amyloid-beta plaques and neurofibrillary tangles leading to neuronal death and cognitive decline.

Disease-Modifying Therapy (DMT): A treatment that alters the underlying course of a neurodegenerative or autoimmune neurological disease rather than merely managing symptoms.

Multiple Sclerosis Relapse: An acute episode of new or worsening neurological symptoms in MS lasting at least 24 hours, caused by inflammatory demyelination in the central nervous system.

Myopathy: A disease of skeletal muscle in which the muscle fibers do not function properly, resulting in proximal weakness, elevated creatine kinase, and characteristic EMG findings.

Neuropathy: A disorder of the peripheral nerves causing weakness, numbness, and pain, classified by distribution (mononeuropathy, polyneuropathy) and pathology (axonal, demyelinating).

Neuromuscular Junction Disorder: A condition such as myasthenia gravis that impairs signal transmission between motor neurons and skeletal muscle at the neuromuscular junction.

Functional Independence Measure (FIM): A standardized rehabilitation outcome scale that assesses a patient's level of disability and need for assistance across motor and cognitive domains.

Modified Rankin Scale (mRS): A clinician-reported measure of the degree of disability or dependence in daily activities following stroke, graded from 0 (no symptoms) to 6 (death).

NIH Stroke Scale (NIHSS): A 15-item standardized assessment tool that quantifies the severity of acute ischemic stroke based on level of consciousness, motor function, sensory function, language, and neglect.

Neuroplasticity: The brain's capacity to reorganize its structure and function in response to injury, learning, or environmental stimulation, forming the biological basis for neurorehabilitation.

DICOM (Digital Imaging and Communications in Medicine): The international standard for transmitting, storing, and sharing medical imaging data including neuroimaging studies.

HL7 FHIR (Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources): A healthcare data exchange standard that defines resources and APIs for interoperability between clinical information systems including neurology records.
