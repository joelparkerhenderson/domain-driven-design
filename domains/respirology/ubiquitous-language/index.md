# Respirology Domain - Ubiquitous Language

Spirometry: a pulmonary function test that measures the volume and flow of air during forced exhalation, producing key metrics such as FEV1 and FVC.

FEV1 (Forced Expiratory Volume in 1 Second): the volume of air a patient can forcibly exhale in one second, used as a primary indicator of airflow obstruction severity.

FVC (Forced Vital Capacity): the total volume of air a patient can forcibly exhale after a maximal inhalation, used alongside FEV1 to calculate the obstruction ratio.

FEV1/FVC Ratio: the proportion of vital capacity expelled in the first second of forced exhalation, where a value below 0.70 typically indicates obstructive airway disease.

Peak Expiratory Flow (PEF): the maximum speed of expiration measured during a forced breath, commonly used in asthma monitoring and action plans.

Pulse Oximetry: a non-invasive measurement of arterial oxygen saturation (SpO2) using a sensor placed on the finger or earlobe.

Arterial Blood Gas (ABG): a laboratory test analyzing arterial blood to determine partial pressures of oxygen and carbon dioxide, pH, and bicarbonate levels.

Bronchoscopy: an endoscopic procedure using a flexible or rigid scope to visually examine the airways, obtain tissue samples, or perform therapeutic interventions.

Bronchoalveolar Lavage (BAL): a diagnostic procedure performed during bronchoscopy in which saline is instilled into a lung segment and then collected for cellular and microbiological analysis.

Dyspnea: the subjective sensation of breathing difficulty or shortness of breath, assessed using standardized scales such as the mMRC or Borg scale.

Auscultation: the clinical technique of listening to lung sounds with a stethoscope to detect abnormalities such as wheezing, crackles, or diminished breath sounds.

COPD (Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease): a progressive lung disease characterized by persistent airflow limitation, classified by the GOLD framework into stages based on spirometric severity and exacerbation risk.

Exacerbation: an acute worsening of respiratory symptoms beyond normal day-to-day variation that requires a change in medication or hospitalization.

GOLD Classification: the Global Initiative for Chronic Obstructive Lung Disease staging system that categorizes COPD severity using spirometry, symptoms, and exacerbation history.

Interstitial Lung Disease (ILD): a group of disorders characterized by progressive scarring (fibrosis) of the lung tissue between the air sacs, affecting gas exchange.

Bronchiectasis: a chronic condition in which the bronchial tubes are permanently dilated and damaged, leading to mucus accumulation and recurrent infections.

Sarcoidosis: a systemic granulomatous disease of unknown cause that frequently involves the lungs and lymph nodes, characterized by non-caseating granulomas.

Non-Invasive Ventilation (NIV): a form of ventilatory support delivered through a mask or nasal interface without endotracheal intubation, commonly using BiPAP or CPAP modes.

Mechanical Ventilation: the use of a ventilator machine to fully or partially support breathing through an endotracheal tube or tracheostomy, with configurable parameters for volume, pressure, and rate.

High-Flow Nasal Therapy (HFNT): the delivery of heated, humidified oxygen at high flow rates through nasal cannulae, providing a degree of positive airway pressure and improved oxygenation.

Intubation: the placement of an endotracheal tube into the trachea to secure the airway and facilitate mechanical ventilation.

Tracheostomy: a surgical procedure creating an opening in the anterior trachea for insertion of a tracheostomy tube, used for prolonged ventilatory support or airway protection.

Airway Clearance Technique: a set of therapeutic methods including chest physiotherapy, postural drainage, oscillatory devices, and cough augmentation used to mobilize and remove bronchial secretions.

Bronchodilator: a medication that relaxes bronchial smooth muscle to widen the airways, classified as short-acting (SABA/SAMA) or long-acting (LABA/LAMA).

Pulmonary Rehabilitation: a comprehensive, evidence-based intervention combining exercise training, breathing retraining, education, and psychosocial support for patients with chronic respiratory disease.

Six-Minute Walk Test (6MWT): a standardized functional exercise test measuring the distance a patient can walk on a flat surface in six minutes, used to assess exercise capacity and treatment response.

mMRC Dyspnea Scale: the Modified Medical Research Council scale that grades breathlessness severity from 0 (breathless only with strenuous exercise) to 4 (too breathless to leave the house).

CAT Score (COPD Assessment Test): an eight-item patient-completed questionnaire that quantifies the impact of COPD on health status, producing a score from 0 to 40.

Action Plan: a written, individualized set of instructions for a patient that specifies how to recognize symptom changes and what self-management steps to take, including when to seek medical attention.

Ventilator Weaning: the gradual process of reducing ventilatory support and transitioning a patient to spontaneous breathing, guided by readiness criteria and structured protocols.

Respiratory Therapist: a healthcare professional specializing in the assessment, treatment, and management of patients with breathing disorders, responsible for administering pulmonary function tests, managing ventilators, and delivering respiratory treatments.
