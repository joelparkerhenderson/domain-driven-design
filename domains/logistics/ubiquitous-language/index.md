# Logistics - Ubiquitous Language

Shipment: A consignment of goods being transported from an origin to a destination through the logistics network.

Consignment: A batch of goods entrusted to a carrier for transportation, identified by a unique consignment number.

Bill of Lading (BOL): A legal document issued by a carrier acknowledging receipt of goods for transport, serving as a contract of carriage and title document.

Freight: Goods transported in bulk by truck, rail, ship, or aircraft, typically categorized by weight, class, and handling requirements.

Carrier: A company or individual that physically transports goods, such as a trucking company, railroad, ocean line, or airline.

Broker: A licensed intermediary who arranges freight transportation between shippers and carriers without owning transportation assets.

Shipper: The party that originates a shipment by tendering goods to a carrier or broker for transportation.

Consignee: The party designated to receive a shipment at the destination.

Load: A quantity of freight assigned to a specific vehicle or transportation unit for a trip.

Full Truckload (FTL): A shipment that fills an entire truck trailer, typically over 10,000 pounds or requiring dedicated vehicle capacity.

Less Than Truckload (LTL): A shipment that occupies only a portion of a truck trailer and is consolidated with other shipments.

Freight Class: A classification system (NMFC) that categorizes commodities based on density, handling, stowability, and liability for LTL pricing.

Route: A planned path from origin to destination consisting of one or more route segments, waypoints, and stops.

Route Segment: A single leg of a multi-stop route between two consecutive waypoints.

Lane: A recurring origin-destination pair used for pricing, capacity planning, and carrier performance analysis.

Deadhead: Empty miles driven by a vehicle without carrying revenue-generating freight.

Dwell Time: The duration a vehicle or trailer remains stationary at a facility, including loading, unloading, and waiting.

Transit Time: The elapsed time from shipment pickup at origin to delivery at destination.

Dispatch: The act of assigning a vehicle and driver to a specific load or route and authorizing departure.

Fleet: The collection of vehicles owned or operated by a logistics company for freight transportation.

Vehicle: A truck, tractor, van, or other motorized unit used to transport freight or pull trailers.

Trailer: A non-motorized transport unit pulled by a tractor, such as a dry van, refrigerated trailer, or flatbed.

Tractor: A powered vehicle designed to pull one or more trailers.

Driver: A licensed individual operating a vehicle to transport freight, subject to hours-of-service regulations.

Hours of Service (HOS): Federal regulations limiting the number of hours a commercial driver may drive and work before mandatory rest.

Electronic Logging Device (ELD): A device attached to a vehicle's engine that automatically records driving time for hours-of-service compliance.

Telematics: Technology systems that collect and transmit vehicle data including GPS position, speed, fuel consumption, and engine diagnostics.

Geofence: A virtual geographic boundary that triggers events when a vehicle enters or exits a defined area.

Proof of Delivery (POD): Documentation confirming successful delivery, including recipient signature, timestamp, and photographic evidence.

Delivery Window: A specified time range during which a delivery must occur, as agreed with the consignee.

Last Mile: The final transportation segment from a distribution hub or depot to the end recipient's address.

Dock Door: A loading or unloading bay at a warehouse or distribution center where trailers are positioned for freight transfer.

Yard: The outdoor area surrounding a warehouse or distribution center where trailers are parked, staged, and moved.

Yard Jockey: A specialized driver who moves trailers within a yard between dock doors, parking spots, and staging areas.

Gate: The controlled entry and exit point of a yard where vehicles are checked in and out.

Dock Appointment: A scheduled time slot for a carrier to arrive at a specific dock door for loading or unloading.

Customs Declaration: A formal document submitted to customs authorities describing imported or exported goods, their value, origin, and classification.

Harmonized System Code (HS Code): An internationally standardized numerical code for classifying traded products, used to determine duty rates.

Incoterm: A standardized trade term published by the International Chamber of Commerce defining responsibilities for shipping, insurance, and customs between buyer and seller.

Duty: A tax or tariff imposed by a government on imported or exported goods based on their classification and country of origin.

Customs Broker: A licensed professional who prepares and submits customs documentation and ensures compliance with import and export regulations.

Bonded Warehouse: A secured warehouse where imported goods may be stored without payment of duty until they are released for domestic consumption or re-exported.

Freight Rate: The price charged by a carrier for transporting a shipment, determined by weight, distance, freight class, and market conditions.

Spot Rate: A one-time freight rate negotiated for an individual shipment, typically when contract capacity is unavailable.

Contract Rate: A pre-negotiated freight rate between a shipper and carrier for a specified lane, volume, and time period.

Load Board: A digital marketplace where brokers and carriers post and search for available loads and truck capacity.

Tender: A formal offer of freight from a shipper or broker to a carrier, specifying origin, destination, pickup time, and rate.

Accessorial Charge: An additional fee for services beyond standard transportation, such as liftgate delivery, detention, or inside pickup.

Detention: A charge assessed when a vehicle is held at a facility beyond the allotted free time for loading or unloading.

Demurrage: A charge for the use of a container or trailer beyond the agreed-upon free time at a port or terminal.

Intermodal: Transportation using two or more modes (truck, rail, ship, air) in a single shipment journey.

Cross-Dock: A logistics practice where inbound freight is unloaded and directly transferred to outbound vehicles with minimal or no storage.

Way Bill: A document accompanying freight that describes the goods, route, and parties involved in the shipment.
