# Psychiatry Domain - Ubiquitous Language

Psychiatric Evaluation: A comprehensive clinical assessment conducted by a psychiatrist to determine a patient's mental health status, diagnosis, and treatment needs.

DSM-5-TR Diagnosis: A standardized diagnostic classification assigned using the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, Fifth Edition, Text Revision, published by the American Psychiatric Association.

Structured Clinical Interview: A systematic, protocol-driven interview method (such as SCID-5) used to reliably assess psychiatric diagnoses according to standardized criteria.

Rating Scale: A validated psychometric instrument (such as PHQ-9, GAD-7, or PANSS) used to quantify the severity of psychiatric symptoms.

Differential Diagnosis: The systematic process of distinguishing a particular psychiatric disorder from others that share similar clinical features.

Psychopharmacology: The branch of psychiatry concerned with the use of medications to treat mental disorders, including mechanism of action, efficacy, and side effects.

Polypharmacy: The concurrent use of multiple psychiatric medications by a single patient, requiring careful management of drug interactions and cumulative side effects.

Therapeutic Drug Monitoring: The clinical practice of measuring medication blood levels to optimize dosing, ensure therapeutic efficacy, and minimize toxicity.

Pharmacogenomics: The study of how a patient's genetic makeup affects their response to psychiatric medications, guiding personalized prescribing decisions.

Prescription: A clinician's formal order for a specific psychiatric medication, including drug name, dosage, route of administration, frequency, and duration.

Psychotherapy: A structured therapeutic intervention delivered by a trained clinician using evidence-based techniques to treat mental health conditions.

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT): An evidence-based psychotherapy modality that focuses on identifying and modifying dysfunctional thoughts, beliefs, and behaviors.

Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT): A specialized form of CBT originally developed for borderline personality disorder that emphasizes mindfulness, distress tolerance, emotion regulation, and interpersonal effectiveness.

Psychodynamic Therapy: A therapeutic modality rooted in psychoanalytic theory that explores unconscious processes, early experiences, and relational patterns to alleviate psychological distress.

Treatment Protocol: A standardized, evidence-based plan specifying the sequence and methods of therapeutic interventions for a particular diagnosis or condition.

Therapeutic Alliance: The collaborative relationship between clinician and patient characterized by mutual trust, agreement on treatment goals, and engagement in therapeutic tasks.

Psychiatric Emergency: An acute clinical situation in which a patient presents an immediate risk of harm to self or others, requiring urgent psychiatric intervention.

Suicide Risk Assessment: A structured clinical evaluation of a patient's risk factors, warning signs, and protective factors to determine the likelihood and imminence of suicidal behavior.

Involuntary Hold: A legal mechanism (such as a 5150 hold) allowing temporary psychiatric detention of an individual who poses a danger to self or others due to a mental disorder.

Safety Plan: A prioritized, written list of coping strategies and resources that a patient can use during a suicidal crisis to reduce the risk of self-harm.

De-escalation: A set of verbal and non-verbal communication techniques used to reduce agitation and prevent escalation to violence in a psychiatric setting.

Psychosocial Rehabilitation: A comprehensive set of interventions aimed at restoring social functioning, daily living skills, and community participation for individuals with serious mental illness.

Supported Employment: A vocational rehabilitation approach that helps individuals with mental illness obtain and maintain competitive employment in integrated community settings.

Cognitive Remediation: A behavioral training intervention targeting cognitive deficits (attention, memory, executive function) commonly associated with serious mental illness.

Clinical Outcome: A measurable change in a patient's health status resulting from psychiatric treatment, assessed using standardized instruments and clinical observation.

Functional Assessment: An evaluation of a patient's ability to perform daily living activities, maintain social relationships, and participate in occupational and community roles.

Patient-Reported Outcome: A health outcome measure directly reported by the patient without clinician interpretation, capturing the patient's perspective on symptoms, functioning, and quality of life.

Treatment Plan: A comprehensive, individualized document specifying psychiatric diagnoses, treatment goals, planned interventions, responsible clinicians, and review timelines.

Comorbidity: The simultaneous presence of two or more psychiatric disorders or medical conditions in a single patient, complicating diagnosis and treatment planning.

Informed Consent: The process by which a clinician provides a patient with sufficient information about a proposed treatment to enable a voluntary and competent decision about participation.

Recovery-Oriented Care: A treatment philosophy that emphasizes patient empowerment, hope, personal responsibility, and meaningful community participation as central goals of psychiatric treatment.
