# Mast Cell Activation Syndrome (MCAS) Domain - Ubiquitous Language

Mast Cell: An immune cell originating from bone marrow that resides in connective tissues and releases chemical mediators in response to stimuli.

Mast Cell Activation Syndrome (MCAS): A condition characterized by episodic excessive mast cell mediator release causing multi-system symptoms without evidence of clonal mast cell disease.

Mediator: A biochemical substance released by mast cells during degranulation, including histamine, tryptase, prostaglandins, and leukotrienes.

Degranulation: The process by which mast cells release their stored chemical mediators into surrounding tissue.

Tryptase: A serine protease enzyme released by mast cells, measured in serum as a biomarker for mast cell activation.

Histamine Metabolite: A breakdown product of histamine, typically N-methylhistamine, measured in urine to assess histamine burden.

Prostaglandin D2: A lipid mediator produced by mast cells, measured as its metabolite 11-beta-prostaglandin F2-alpha in urine.

Consensus Criteria: A standardized set of diagnostic requirements established by expert panels for confirming an MCAS diagnosis.

Trigger: Any stimulus that provokes mast cell degranulation, including foods, environmental exposures, medications, stress, or physical factors.

Avoidance Strategy: A personalized plan to minimize exposure to identified triggers through environmental, dietary, and behavioral modifications.

Environmental Control: Measures taken to reduce exposure to airborne or contact triggers such as fragrances, chemicals, temperature extremes, and allergens.

Dietary Management: A structured approach to identifying and avoiding food-related triggers while maintaining adequate nutrition.

H1 Antihistamine: A medication that blocks histamine H1 receptors to reduce symptoms such as itching, flushing, and hives.

H2 Antihistamine: A medication that blocks histamine H2 receptors to reduce gastrointestinal symptoms including acid reflux and abdominal pain.

Mast Cell Stabilizer: A medication such as cromolyn sodium or ketotifen that prevents mast cell degranulation.

Leukotriene Inhibitor: A medication such as montelukast or zileuton that blocks the effects of leukotrienes released during mast cell activation.

Compounded Medication: A custom-prepared medication formulated without dyes, fillers, or excipients that may trigger mast cell reactions.

Titration Plan: A structured schedule for gradually increasing or adjusting medication dosages to achieve therapeutic benefit while minimizing adverse reactions.

Flare: An acute episode of intensified mast cell activation symptoms, often triggered by an identifiable stimulus or occurring spontaneously.

Severity Score: A numerical rating assigned to a symptom or flare event to quantify its intensity and impact on daily functioning.

Symptom Burden: The cumulative impact of all active symptoms on a patient's daily life, measured across multiple organ systems.

Multi-System Symptom: A clinical presentation involving simultaneous symptoms across two or more organ systems, characteristic of MCAS.

Pattern Analysis: The systematic review of symptom logs to identify recurring triggers, temporal patterns, and treatment response trends.

Shared Care Plan: A coordinated treatment plan accessible to all providers involved in a patient's MCAS management.

Referral: A formal request from one healthcare provider to another specialist for evaluation, consultation, or co-management.

Quality of Life Score: A standardized metric assessing the overall impact of MCAS on a patient's physical, emotional, and social well-being.

Functional Status: A measure of a patient's ability to perform daily activities, work, and social participation despite MCAS symptoms.

Medication Effectiveness: An assessment of how well a specific medication or regimen reduces symptom burden and improves functional outcomes.

Bone Marrow Biopsy: A diagnostic procedure that examines bone marrow tissue to differentiate MCAS from clonal mast cell disorders such as mastocytosis.

Baseline Mediator Level: The measured level of a mast cell mediator when a patient is not experiencing a flare, used as a comparison point for episodic elevations.
