# Hearing Aid Domain - Ubiquitous Language

- **Acclimatization**: The gradual process by which a new hearing aid user adapts to amplified sound over a period of weeks, often managed through incremental gain increases.

- **Air Conduction Threshold**: The softest sound level at which a patient can detect a pure tone presented through headphones or insert earphones, measured in decibels hearing level (dB HL) across standard audiometric frequencies.

- **Audiogram**: A graphical representation of hearing sensitivity plotted as frequency (Hz) on the horizontal axis and hearing threshold level (dB HL) on the vertical axis for each ear.

- **Behind-the-Ear (BTE)**: A hearing aid style in which the electronic components are housed in a case worn behind the pinna, connected to an earmold or dome in the ear canal.

- **Bone Conduction Threshold**: The softest sound level at which a patient can detect a pure tone delivered through a bone oscillator placed on the mastoid or forehead, bypassing the outer and middle ear.

- **Compression Ratio**: The input/output relationship in a hearing aid's automatic gain control system that determines how much the output level changes for a given change in input level.

- **Directional Microphone**: A microphone system in a hearing aid that preferentially amplifies sounds arriving from the front while attenuating sounds from other directions, improving speech understanding in noise.

- **Earmold**: A custom-shaped coupling device made from an ear impression that channels amplified sound from a hearing aid into the ear canal while providing acoustic sealing and retention.

- **Feedback Cancellation**: A signal processing algorithm in a hearing aid that detects and suppresses acoustic feedback (whistling) caused by amplified sound leaking from the ear canal back to the microphone.

- **Fitting Formula**: A prescriptive algorithm (such as NAL-NL2 or DSL v5.0) that calculates frequency-specific gain and output targets based on audiometric thresholds to optimize speech audibility.

- **Gain**: The amount of amplification provided by a hearing aid at a given frequency, measured as the difference in decibels between the input sound level and the output sound level.

- **Maximum Power Output (MPO)**: The highest sound pressure level a hearing aid can produce regardless of input level, set to protect the wearer from uncomfortably loud sounds.

- **Noise Reduction**: A digital signal processing feature in hearing aids that identifies and attenuates non-speech environmental noise to improve listening comfort without significantly affecting speech signals.

- **Occlusion Effect**: The perception of increased loudness of one's own voice caused by a hearing aid or earmold blocking the ear canal, trapping low-frequency bone-conducted sound energy.

- **Real-Ear Measurement (REM)**: An objective verification procedure using a probe microphone placed in the ear canal to measure the actual sound pressure level delivered by a hearing aid at the tympanic membrane.

- **Receiver-in-Canal (RIC)**: A hearing aid style in which the receiver (speaker) is placed directly in the ear canal via a thin wire, with remaining components housed in a small case behind the ear.

- **Sensorineural Hearing Loss**: Hearing loss resulting from damage to the cochlear hair cells or the auditory nerve, typically permanent and the most common type addressed by hearing aid amplification.

- **Speech-in-Noise Test**: An audiological assessment that measures a patient's ability to understand speech presented in the presence of competing background noise, quantified as the signal-to-noise ratio required for criterion performance.

- **Telecoil**: An inductive pickup coil inside a hearing aid that receives electromagnetic signals from hearing loop systems, enabling direct audio input in equipped venues without acoustic pickup.

- **Tympanometry**: An objective test that measures the compliance (mobility) of the tympanic membrane and middle ear system as a function of air pressure changes in the sealed ear canal.

- **Vent**: A channel drilled through an earmold or hearing aid shell that allows air and low-frequency sound to pass in and out of the ear canal, reducing the occlusion effect and modifying the acoustic response.
