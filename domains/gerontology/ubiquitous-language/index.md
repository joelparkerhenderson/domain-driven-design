# Gerontology Domain - Ubiquitous Language

Comprehensive Geriatric Assessment (CGA): A multidimensional, interdisciplinary diagnostic process that determines the medical, psychological, and functional capabilities and limitations of an older adult.

Frailty Index: A quantitative measure of accumulated health deficits that predicts adverse outcomes such as hospitalization, disability, and mortality in older adults.

Fall Risk Assessment: A structured evaluation of an older adult's likelihood of falling, incorporating balance, gait, medication review, and environmental hazard analysis.

Geriatric Syndrome: A collection of clinical conditions common in older adults that do not fit into discrete disease categories, including falls, delirium, incontinence, and frailty.

Interdisciplinary Care Team: A group of healthcare professionals from different disciplines who collaborate to develop and implement a unified care plan for an older adult.

Care Transition: The movement of a patient between healthcare settings or levels of care, such as from hospital to skilled nursing facility or from facility to home.

Caregiver Burden: The physical, emotional, and financial strain experienced by informal caregivers providing support to older adults with chronic conditions or disabilities.

Community Resource: External services and programs available to support older adults, including transportation, meal delivery, adult day programs, and home health services.

Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE): A 30-point screening tool that measures cognitive function across orientation, registration, attention, recall, and language domains.

Montreal Cognitive Assessment (MoCA): A 30-point cognitive screening instrument designed to detect mild cognitive impairment with higher sensitivity than the MMSE.

Dementia Staging: The classification of dementia severity into stages (mild, moderate, severe) based on cognitive and functional decline, guiding care planning and resource allocation.

Memory Care Plan: A structured care approach tailored to individuals with cognitive impairment, addressing safety, behavioral management, caregiver education, and quality of life.

Activities of Daily Living (ADL): Basic self-care tasks that include bathing, dressing, toileting, transferring, continence, and feeding, measured by instruments such as the Katz Index.

Instrumental Activities of Daily Living (IADL): Higher-order tasks necessary for independent community living, including managing finances, medication, transportation, housekeeping, and meal preparation.

Functional Status: The overall ability of an older adult to perform daily activities independently, serving as a key predictor of health outcomes, care needs, and quality of life.

Assistive Technology: Devices and systems that help older adults maintain independence, including grab bars, walkers, hearing aids, medication dispensers, and smart home sensors.

Home Modification: Physical changes made to a living environment to improve safety and accessibility for older adults, such as ramp installation, bathroom rails, and improved lighting.

Polypharmacy: The concurrent use of five or more medications by an older adult, which increases the risk of adverse drug reactions, drug interactions, and medication non-adherence.

Beers Criteria: An evidence-based list published by the American Geriatrics Society identifying potentially inappropriate medications for older adults due to heightened risk of adverse effects.

Deprescribing: The systematic process of identifying and discontinuing medications where the potential harms outweigh the benefits, guided by patient goals and clinical evidence.

Medication Reconciliation: The process of comparing a patient's current medication orders against all medications the patient is actually taking to avoid errors during care transitions.

Medication Adherence: The extent to which a patient takes medications as prescribed, influenced by regimen complexity, cognitive status, side effects, and caregiver support.

Advance Directive: A legal document in which a person specifies their preferences for medical treatment in the event they become unable to make decisions for themselves.

Palliative Care: Specialized medical care focused on providing relief from symptoms, pain, and stress of serious illness, with the goal of improving quality of life for both patient and family.

Hospice Care: A model of end-of-life care that provides comfort-focused treatment and support services when curative treatment is no longer pursued, typically for patients with a prognosis of six months or less.

Goals of Care Conversation: A structured discussion between clinicians, patients, and families to align medical treatment plans with the patient's values, preferences, and desired outcomes.

POLST (Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment): A portable medical order form that translates a patient's treatment wishes into actionable clinical orders honored across care settings.

Sarcopenia: The age-related progressive loss of skeletal muscle mass and strength, contributing to frailty, functional decline, and increased fall risk in older adults.

Delirium: An acute, fluctuating disturbance in attention and cognition that is common in hospitalized older adults and often triggered by infection, medication, or metabolic imbalance.

Geriatric Care Manager: A healthcare professional, typically a nurse or social worker, who specializes in assessing, planning, and coordinating care for older adults and their families.
