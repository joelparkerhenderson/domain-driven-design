# Hematology Domain - Ubiquitous Language

- **Anemia**: A condition characterized by a reduction in the number of red blood cells or hemoglobin concentration below the normal range, resulting in decreased oxygen-carrying capacity of the blood.

- **Anticoagulation Therapy**: The administration of medications (warfarin, heparin, direct oral anticoagulants) that inhibit the coagulation cascade to prevent or treat thrombotic events.

- **Bone Marrow Biopsy**: A diagnostic procedure in which a core sample of bone marrow tissue is obtained, typically from the posterior iliac crest, for histological examination of cellularity, architecture, and pathology.

- **Coagulation Cascade**: The sequential activation of clotting factors through intrinsic and extrinsic pathways that converge to form a stable fibrin clot at the site of vascular injury.

- **Complete Blood Count (CBC)**: A fundamental laboratory test that quantifies the cellular components of blood, including red blood cells, white blood cells, hemoglobin, hematocrit, and platelets.

- **Crossmatch**: A pre-transfusion compatibility test that mixes donor red blood cells with recipient serum to detect potentially harmful antibodies that could cause a hemolytic transfusion reaction.

- **D-Dimer**: A fibrin degradation product measured in blood whose elevation indicates active clot formation and breakdown, used as a screening test for disseminated intravascular coagulation and venous thromboembolism.

- **Engraftment**: The process by which transplanted hematopoietic stem cells establish themselves in the recipient's bone marrow and begin producing new blood cells, typically defined by sustained neutrophil recovery above 500 cells per microliter.

- **Flow Cytometry**: A laboratory technique that uses laser-based detection of fluorescently labeled antibodies to identify, count, and characterize individual cells based on their surface and intracellular protein expression.

- **Graft-Versus-Host Disease (GVHD)**: A complication of allogeneic bone marrow transplantation in which donor immune cells attack the recipient's tissues, most commonly affecting the skin, liver, and gastrointestinal tract.

- **Hematocrit**: The percentage of blood volume occupied by red blood cells, determined by centrifugation or calculated from the red blood cell count and mean corpuscular volume.

- **Hemoglobin**: The iron-containing protein in red blood cells responsible for binding and transporting oxygen from the lungs to peripheral tissues and returning carbon dioxide to the lungs.

- **Hemoglobinopathy**: A group of inherited disorders caused by structural abnormalities or reduced production of hemoglobin, including sickle cell disease and thalassemia syndromes.

- **HLA Typing**: The laboratory determination of human leukocyte antigen alleles on chromosome 6, essential for matching donors and recipients in bone marrow and solid organ transplantation.

- **International Normalized Ratio (INR)**: A standardized measure of the prothrombin time that corrects for variations in thromboplastin reagents, used to monitor warfarin anticoagulation therapy.

- **Leukemia**: A group of hematologic malignancies characterized by the uncontrolled proliferation of abnormal white blood cells in the bone marrow and peripheral blood, classified as acute or chronic and lymphoid or myeloid.

- **Lymphoma**: A malignant neoplasm arising from lymphocytes within the lymphatic system, broadly categorized as Hodgkin lymphoma or non-Hodgkin lymphoma based on the presence or absence of Reed-Sternberg cells.

- **Mean Corpuscular Volume (MCV)**: The average volume of a single red blood cell measured in femtoliters, used to classify anemias as microcytic, normocytic, or macrocytic.

- **Multiple Myeloma**: A hematologic malignancy characterized by the clonal proliferation of plasma cells in the bone marrow producing monoclonal immunoglobulin, leading to bone destruction, renal impairment, and cytopenias.

- **Neutropenia**: A reduction in the absolute neutrophil count below 1,500 cells per microliter, increasing susceptibility to bacterial and fungal infections.

- **Platelet**: An anucleate cell fragment derived from megakaryocytes in the bone marrow that plays a central role in primary hemostasis through adhesion, activation, and aggregation at sites of vascular injury.

- **Thrombocytopenia**: A condition defined by a platelet count below 150,000 per microliter, which may result from decreased production, increased destruction, or sequestration, and increases the risk of bleeding.

- **Thrombophilia**: An inherited or acquired predisposition to excessive blood clot formation, caused by conditions such as Factor V Leiden mutation, prothrombin gene mutation, or antiphospholipid syndrome.

- **Transfusion Reaction**: An adverse event occurring during or after the administration of a blood product, ranging from mild febrile or allergic reactions to life-threatening acute hemolytic or transfusion-related acute lung injury (TRALI).

- **Von Willebrand Disease**: The most common inherited bleeding disorder, caused by quantitative or qualitative deficiency of von Willebrand factor, a glycoprotein essential for platelet adhesion and Factor VIII stabilization.
