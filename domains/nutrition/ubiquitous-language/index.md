# Nutrition Domain - Ubiquitous Language

Anthropometry: The measurement of the human body's physical dimensions and composition, including height, weight, BMI, skinfold thickness, and waist circumference, used in nutritional status assessment.

Biomarker: A measurable biological indicator, such as serum albumin or hemoglobin, that reflects nutritional status or the physiological effect of dietary intake.

Body Composition: The proportional distribution of fat mass, lean mass, bone mineral content, and water within the body, tracked to evaluate the effectiveness of nutrition interventions.

Caloric Balance: The relationship between energy intake from food and energy expenditure through metabolism and physical activity, determining weight gain, loss, or maintenance.

Compliance Score: A quantitative measure of how closely an individual's actual dietary intake aligns with their prescribed or recommended dietary plan over a specified period.

Dietary Pattern: A recurring configuration of food choices and eating behaviors, such as Mediterranean, DASH, ketogenic, or plant-based, that defines an individual's overall nutritional approach.

Dietary Recall: A structured interview method, typically covering 24 hours, in which an individual reports all foods and beverages consumed, used for nutritional assessment.

Dietary Reference Intake (DRI): A set of scientifically established nutrient intake values published by national health authorities, including Recommended Dietary Allowances (RDA) and Tolerable Upper Intake Levels (UL).

Disease-Specific Diet: A therapeutic dietary prescription tailored to manage or treat a specific medical condition, such as a renal diet for chronic kidney disease or a cardiac diet for cardiovascular disease.

Energy Availability: The amount of dietary energy remaining for physiological functions after subtracting the energy cost of exercise, a critical metric in sports nutrition for preventing relative energy deficiency.

Enteral Nutrition: The delivery of nutrients directly into the gastrointestinal tract via tube feeding, used when oral intake is insufficient but the gut is functional.

Food Composition Database: A standardized reference containing the nutrient content of foods, such as the USDA FoodData Central, used as the foundation for nutrient analysis calculations.

Food Frequency Questionnaire (FFQ): An assessment instrument that measures habitual dietary intake over an extended period by asking about the frequency and portion size of specific food items.

Food Log Entry: A discrete record of a food or beverage consumed at a specific time, including item identification, quantity, preparation method, and the meal occasion.

Grocery List: An itemized list of ingredients derived from a meal plan, aggregated by food category and adjusted for household inventory, used to support meal plan execution.

Hydration Protocol: A structured plan specifying fluid type, volume, and timing relative to physical activity, designed to maintain optimal hydration status during training or competition.

Macronutrient: One of the three primary energy-yielding nutrients: protein, carbohydrate, and fat, each quantified in grams and contributing to total caloric intake.

Meal Plan: A structured schedule of meals and snacks over a defined period, designed to meet specific nutritional targets and dietary preferences for an individual or population.

Medical Nutrition Therapy (MNT): An evidence-based application of the Nutrition Care Process to treat medical conditions through individualized dietary assessment, intervention, and monitoring, typically delivered by a registered dietitian.

Micronutrient: A vitamin or mineral required in small amounts for normal physiological function, including iron, calcium, vitamin D, and B-complex vitamins, often assessed for adequacy against DRI values.

Nutrient Analysis: The computational process of determining the macro and micronutrient content of a food intake record by matching consumed items against a food composition database.

Nutrition Care Process (NCP): A systematic framework consisting of nutrition assessment, diagnosis, intervention, and monitoring/evaluation, standardized by the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics for clinical practice.

Nutrition Diagnosis: A formal identification of a nutrition problem using the International Dietetics and Nutrition Terminology (IDNT), structured as problem, etiology, and signs/symptoms (PES statement).

Parenteral Nutrition: The intravenous delivery of nutrients directly into the bloodstream, bypassing the gastrointestinal tract, used when enteral feeding is contraindicated.

Patient-Reported Outcome: A subjective measure of health status or quality of life reported directly by the patient, such as perceived energy level, appetite, satiety, or gastrointestinal comfort.

Periodized Fueling: A sports nutrition strategy that adjusts macronutrient intake and meal timing to align with training phases (base, build, peak, recovery) within an athletic periodization plan.

Recipe: A formalized set of instructions combining specific ingredients in defined quantities with preparation and cooking methods, associated with a calculated nutritional profile.

RED-S (Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport): A syndrome resulting from insufficient energy availability relative to the demands of exercise, leading to impaired physiological function across metabolic, reproductive, bone, and immune systems.

Supplementation Regimen: A planned schedule of dietary supplements, including type, dosage, timing, and duration, prescribed to address identified nutrient deficiencies or support performance goals.

Tolerable Upper Intake Level (UL): The maximum daily intake of a nutrient that is unlikely to cause adverse health effects, established as part of the Dietary Reference Intake framework.
